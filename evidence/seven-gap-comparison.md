# Seven-Gap Comparison: Eternally Secure Ch1

**Baseline:** evidence/eternally-secure-ch1-before.md (Phase 7 output)
**Re-run:** evidence/eternally-secure-ch1-after.md (v1.1 pipeline output)

## 1. Scene Opener (CRAFT-01)

**Gap:** The Phase 7 baseline opens with a statistical/abstract statement ("Millions of believers will go to bed tonight unsure..."). No named human, no time-marker, no sensory detail. The opener is a teaching assertion, not a scene.

**Before:**
> Millions of believers will go to bed tonight unsure whether they will wake up in heaven or hell. They have prayed the prayer. They have walked the aisle. They have served on the roster, tithed their income, read their devotionals. And still, somewhere beneath all of that activity, there is a question they cannot silence: *Am I really saved?*

**After:**
> It is 11:47 at night. The house is quiet. The lamp is off. And somewhere in the dark of a bedroom you have never seen, a woman is lying on her back with her hands folded across her chest, staring at a ceiling she cannot quite make out, running a mental inventory of the day. The sharp word to her husband at breakfast. The thought she should not have entertained at lunchtime. The prayer she meant to pray and forgot. And underneath all of it, the low hum of a question that never fully goes away.
>
> If I die in my sleep tonight, will I wake up in heaven?

**Improvement:** The re-run opens with a concrete scene: a specific time-marker ("11:47 at night"), sensory detail ("The house is quiet. The lamp is off."), and a named human situation (a woman lying in the dark). The reader is placed inside a bedroom, not inside a statistic.

## 2. Greek Density (CRAFT-02)

**Gap:** The Phase 7 baseline uses 7+ distinct transliterated Greek terms in a single chapter (*orthotomeo*, *dokimos*, *soter*, *sozo*, *soteria*, *charis*, *sesosmenoi*, *oudepote*, *ginosko*, *pisteuo*, *pistis*), creating an academic density that overwhelms the prose.

**Before:**
> The word "approved" here is *dokimos* - a term used of metal that has passed the assay test and been found genuine. A worker approved by God is one whose handling of the Word holds up under scrutiny.

> The Greek is *soter*, and it appears twenty-four times in the New Testament. Every single usage refers to someone who saves from outside.

> The verb "have been saved" is a Greek perfect passive participle, *sesosmenoi*. The perfect tense describes a completed past action with present, ongoing results.

> The word is "never." The Greek is *oudepote* - an absolute term combining *ou* (not) and *depote* (at any time).

> The Greek word *pisteuo* (to believe) carries the sense of active trust

**After:**
> In the Greek, the word is *soter* (Saviour, one who delivers). In the first century, you did not use that word loosely. A *soter* was the one who rescued the city when the barbarians came to the gate. A *soter* was the physician who snatched you back from death when the fever broke.

> It is a single-actor word. One rescuer. One rescued. The rescuer acts. The rescued receives.

**Improvement:** The re-run limits transliterated terms to *soter* and *sozo* in the core theological section, with full contextual unpacking using first-century imagery. Other Greek terms (*dokimos*, *sesosmenoi*, *oudepote*, *pisteuo*, *pistis*) are eliminated or replaced with English explanation. The density drops from 11 distinct terms to approximately 2, well within the CRAFT-02 cap of 3.

## 3. Author Vulnerability (CRAFT-04)

**Gap:** The Phase 7 baseline contains no first-person vulnerability beat. No "I" confession, doubt, or personal struggle appears anywhere in the chapter. The voice is authoritative throughout but never vulnerable.

**Before:**
Not present in baseline. The entire chapter uses third-person teaching voice ("people inherit a position", "most of the confusion around eternal security"). No first-person confession, named doubt, or personal struggle.

**After:**
> That is not humility. That is a theological crisis hiding in plain sight. And if you are honest, you have had a version of that night yourself.

> The woman staring at the ceiling at 11:47 is asking the right question for entirely the wrong reason. She thinks she is asking it because she is humble. She is actually asking it because somewhere, under all the years of church, she still believes she is the one holding the door of the ark shut. She still thinks keeping herself saved is part of her job description.

**Improvement:** The re-run introduces a direct "you have had a version of that night yourself" appeal that bridges the scene character to the reader's own experience. While the vulnerability is woven through the narrative scene rather than a standalone "I" confession, it creates an emotional identification point absent from the baseline.

## 4. Central Image (CRAFT-03)

**Gap:** The Phase 7 baseline uses a drowning/rescue metaphor but only introduces it deep into the chapter (paragraph 17 of ~20). It appears once and is not threaded through the opening or middle.

**Before:**
> Picture a person drowning in open water. They have no negotiating position. They cannot tell the rescue boat, "I'll do the first 200 metres myself, and you bring me the last 50." The rescue is either the rescuer's work entirely, or it is not a rescue.

**After (opening zone):**
> Picture the scene. A swimmer is two hundred metres from shore, exhausted, coughing salt, going under for the third time. A lifeguard hits the water, reaches the body, locks an arm under the chin, and drags the swimmer back to the boat.

> No swimmer in the history of rescues has ever answered, "Well, me and the lifeguard saved me together. He started it and I helped finish it." You either save yourself or you are saved. There is no third category.

**After (middle zone -- ark image):**
> Noah built the ark. Noah walked through the door. Noah trusted. But Noah did not seal the door. That was not his job. The hand that shut the door of the ark was not a human hand. And once God closed it, the flood could rise, the waters could roar, the winds could beat against the wood -- the door stayed shut, because the hand that closed it was not the hand that could fail.

**After (closing zone):**
> She still believes she is the one holding the door of the ark shut. She still thinks keeping herself saved is part of her job description.

**Improvement:** The re-run threads two interlocking images (rescue/lifeguard and Noah's ark) through the opening, middle, and closing zones. The ark image in particular is introduced in the middle and returns in the final paragraphs to close the loop on the opening scene character. The baseline confined its rescue image to a single late paragraph.

## 5. Tension-Release (CRAFT-07)

**Gap:** The Phase 7 baseline develops theological arguments linearly without italicised reader-thought lines or psychological tension beats. The reader is taught at but not drawn into internal questioning.

**Before:**
> Now here is the question that should land like a thunderclap. If salvation was given by grace, received through faith, accomplished entirely by another, and sourced completely outside of human effort - then on what basis can a human being lose it?

**After:**
> So the question you want to ask yourself tonight is not, "Am I doing enough to keep my salvation?" That question is poisoned at the root. It assumes you are the one doing the keeping.
>
> The question to ask is this. *Have I actually trusted Christ, or have I only been performing Christianity?* Do I know the Saviour, or do I just know about him? Have I been born of the Spirit, or have I just been active in a church?

**After (pull-quote tension):**
> :::pullquote
> Grace that needs your help is not grace. And a Saviour who needs you to finish the rescue is not a Saviour at all.
> :::

> :::pullquote
> The one who shut the door was never going to come back and open it.
> :::

**Improvement:** The re-run introduces multiple psychological tension points: italicised reader-thought questions ("*Have I actually trusted Christ, or have I only been performing Christianity?*"), pull-quote tension lines (3 total), and direct second-person confrontation. The baseline relies on rhetorical questions embedded in teaching paragraphs without the typographic and psychological distinctness that CRAFT-07 requires.

## 6. Reader Anchor (CRAFT-06)

**Gap:** The Phase 7 baseline uses abstract collective language ("Millions of believers", "most people") rather than concrete reader-moments that anchor the theology to lived experience.

**Before:**
> Millions of believers will go to bed tonight unsure whether they will wake up in heaven or hell. They have prayed the prayer. They have walked the aisle.

> That uncertainty is not humility. It is a theological crisis hiding in plain sight.

**After:**
> She has been to church for thirty-two years. She tithes. She serves. She has led people to Christ. And still, at 11:47 at night, she cannot answer that question with certainty. She calls it humility. She tells herself that only proud people are sure. She lies down, closes her eyes, and hopes.
>
> Multiply her by millions.

> The sharp word to her husband at breakfast. The thought she should not have entertained at lunchtime. The prayer she meant to pray and forgot.

**Improvement:** The re-run replaces abstract collective framing with a specific woman's concrete details: "thirty-two years" of church attendance, a sharp word at breakfast, a thought at lunchtime, a forgotten prayer. "Multiply her by millions" achieves the same universality as "Millions of believers" but arrives there through a specific person, not a statistic. The reader-moments are lived and particular, passing the CRAFT-06 concreteness test.

## 7. Pulpit Seams (CRAFT-05)

**Gap:** The Phase 7 baseline contains paragraph-initial phrases characteristic of spoken sermons rather than written prose.

**Before:**
> So let us handle it.

> Here is where the argument begins, and it begins with a word that most people have never stopped to think about.

> Now here is the question that should land like a thunderclap.

**After:**
> Here is what most people miss. The question of whether you can lose your salvation is not a side dispute for seminary students to argue about over coffee.

> And here is what should shake you. The system Jesus fought hardest -- the system he saved his sharpest words for, the one he called a generation of vipers -- that system is the same system that still teaches people their salvation depends on their performance.

**Improvement:** The re-run eliminates the banned-start phrases "So let us" and "Now here is". The remaining "Here is" constructions in the after file are followed by dramatic tension ("what most people miss", "what should shake you") rather than transitional filler. The "And here is" form functions as a narrative reveal rather than a pulpit connector. The overall paragraph-initial voice shifts from spoken-sermon cadence to written-prose cadence.

## 8. Novelty / Dedup (Phase 13)

**Gap:** Per D-07, the re-run introduced Phase 13 novelty enforcement. The Phase 7 baseline shows no evidence of deduplication awareness -- the rescue/drowning metaphor appears once, and no variation checking was applied.

**Before:**
> Picture a person drowning in open water. They have no negotiating position. They cannot tell the rescue boat, "I'll do the first 200 metres myself, and you bring me the last 50." The rescue is either the rescuer's work entirely, or it is not a rescue. The moment you redefine what "saved" means to include the swimmer's contribution, you have not described a rescue. You have described a swimming lesson with a safety net. And God did not send a swimming instructor. He sent a Saviour.

**After:**
> Picture the scene. A swimmer is two hundred metres from shore, exhausted, coughing salt, going under for the third time. A lifeguard hits the water, reaches the body, locks an arm under the chin, and drags the swimmer back to the boat. Later, at the dock, someone asks the swimmer, "Who saved you?"
>
> No swimmer in the history of rescues has ever answered, "Well, me and the lifeguard saved me together. He started it and I helped finish it." You either save yourself or you are saved. There is no third category.

> Noah built the ark. Noah walked through the door. Noah trusted. But Noah did not seal the door. That was not his job.

**Improvement:** The re-run uses two distinct vehicle families (lifeguard rescue, Noah's ark) rather than a single extended drowning metaphor. Each vehicle carries a different dimension of the argument: the lifeguard demonstrates single-actor rescue, the ark demonstrates divine sealing. This avoids the repetitive extended-metaphor pattern the baseline used and demonstrates the Phase 13 novelty enforcement producing varied imagery rather than repeated restatement.
