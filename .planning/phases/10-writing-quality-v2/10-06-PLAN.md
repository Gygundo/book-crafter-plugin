    # Bestseller Calibration

    > Paraphrased before/after exemplars for captivation rubric scoring at levels 3, 6, and 9. Editor reads this file during Pass 1/Pass 2 judgment sub-sections to calibrate scoring. All prose is paraphrased — never verbatim from copyrighted sources.

    ## Score Level 3

    A chapter that scores 3/14 on the rubric looks like this (pre-craft, sermon-like):

    **Opening paragraph (score 3 — statistical opener, no scene, no provenance):**

    > Three out of four believers struggle with assurance of salvation at some point in their walk. This is not a small problem. Let us consider what the scripture teaches about grace and identity...

    Why this scores 3: no named human, no time marker, no sensory detail; abstract vocabulary only (believers, salvation, walk, grace, identity); pulpit-seam transition ("Let us consider"); zero vulnerability; no central image.

    ## Score Level 6

    A chapter that scores 6/14 on the rubric looks like this (mid-upgrade, some craft):

    **Opening paragraph (score 6 — named human, time marker, partial sensory, still abstract-heavy):**

    > Sarah checked her phone at 2am. She had been reading John 10 for an hour, hoping one verse would finally feel true. The words on the screen were familiar. The doubt was familiar too. Grace, the pastor had said on Sunday, is a gift. But what if the gift hadn't been given to her?

    Why this scores 6: named human (Sarah), time marker (2am), sensory detail (phone screen, words); reader-thought line ("But what if...") — but central image not yet threaded, no vulnerability beat from author, abstract nouns still outnumber concrete.

    ## Score Level 9

    A chapter that scores 9/14 on the rubric looks like this (bestseller-track):

    **Opening paragraph (score 9 — scene-first, concrete, central image seeded, vulnerability foreshadowed):**

    > The light from Sarah's phone made her kitchen look like a hospital corridor — cold, blue, too bright for 2am. She had read John 10 four times. The fourth read was the worst, because by then the words had stopped meaning anything. She set the phone face-down on the counter, and the sound of it hitting the laminate was louder than it should have been.
    >
    > *But what if the gift hadn't been given to her?*
    >
    > I know that 2am. I have a different kitchen, a different verse, and the same silence.

    Why this scores 9: named human + time marker + three sensory details (light colour, sound of phone on counter, silence); central image seeded (cold hospital-corridor light — will recur in middle and closing); italicised reader-thought line; first-person author vulnerability beat ("I know that 2am"); zero pulpit-seam phrases; concrete:abstract ratio well above 1:1.
